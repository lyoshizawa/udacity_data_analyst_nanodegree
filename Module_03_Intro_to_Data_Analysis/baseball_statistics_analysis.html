Baseball Data Analysis¶

Introduction¶

In 1994, Sean Lahman created a database which contains the pitching, hitting, and fielding statistics for Major League Baseball. Today his database contains baseball statistics from 1871 through 2014, and has expanded to include other information, such as awards earned, Hall of Fame players, player salaries, and which colleges players attended.

With such comprehensive information readily available, many questions can be asked and answered by analyzing this data. I have posed one question which can be answered with this data:

Question: Do left-handed pitchers strike out batters more often than right-handed pitchers? Is there a relationship between strikeouts and number of games played?

To explore this question, we will need to look at two data sets that Sean Lahman created: player information and pitching statistics.

This information is a good start, however there are still a few issues with the data:

1.) It only reflects the raw numbers. Pitchers with longer careers will have higher numbers. Since we are comparing the performance between left-handed and right-handed pitchers, the number of strikeouts is not as important as the number of strikeouts per inning outs.
2.) G is the number of games played and GS is the number of games started. The descriptive statistics shows that at least 25% of baseball pitchers have played in less than 8 games, and didn't start in any!
3.) There is the possibility of missing statistics from players. This will cause an issue with data analysis if a player is not included in every calculation.
4.) There are players who have an 'IPout' value of 0. Since our analysis will look at comparing the strikeouts per inning outs, pitchers should have played for at least one inning out.

#create a function to standardize values
def standardize (df, column):
    df[column+'_std'] = (df[column] - df[column].mean()) / np.std(df[column])
    return df

Conclusions and Findings¶

In the introduction, two questions were asked regarding Sean Lahman's data:

Do left-handed pitchers strike out batters more often than right-handed pitchers? Is there a relationship between strikeouts and number of games played?

To explore these questions, player information and pitching statistics were processed and refined to produce the following information: player name, games played, games started, inning outs played, strikeouts, and throwing hand. There was certainly some surprising information from the basic statistics of these players.

The above information summaries compares the left handed with the right handed players. There are a few statistics to note:

1.) The average right handed pitcher is slightly below the overall average. This is represented with the mean value of -.013227. The average left handed pitcher is slightly above the overall average, with a mean value of .096459. This means that the average left handed player has a performance of .1 standard deviations greater than that of a right handed player.
2.) When looking at the interquartile range, or the middle 50% of players in both groups, it appears the left handed pitchers also performed better. The right handed pitchers fall between -.666 and +.566 standard deviations. The left handed pitchers fell between -.541 and + .670 standard deviations. These statistics indicate that middle group of left handed pitchers perform .104 to .125 better than right handed pitchers.

While this may indicate that left handed pitchers have a better strikeout performance than right handed pitchers, the advantage is very small, by about .1 standard deviations. A T-Test should be performed to see if left handed pitchers do in fact have an advantage over right handed pitchers:

With T-statistic of 4.677 and a P value of .00000294, this is below the .01 threshold for significance. This indicates that left handed pitchers do have an advantage over right handed pitchers, however this does not necessarily mean that left handed pitchers are better at striking out batters. There are other factors which need to be considered, such as which hand the batters hit with.

The scatter plot above shows several interesting things. The trendlines for both left and right handed players are almost identical, indicating that the throwing hand of a pitcher does not appear to be related. The trendline slopes upward, which shows that the more games a player has pitched, the more strikeouts per inning out thrown. This makes sense, as pitchers who perform well are going to be used in more games. However this doesn't necessarily mean this is the direct cause of the relationship shown. Lets look at the Pearson's R coefficient to determine if these two statistics are indeed correlated.

Out[32]:

The Pearson's R coefficient for both sets is roughly .2 with a very small p-value. This indicates a weak positive correlation between the number of games played and the strikeout performance of a pitcher. There are many other factors which can affect the career length of a player, such as injury or the number of hits or runs, so the career of a pitcher is not necessarily reliant upon strikeout performance.

One point to note is that there are a few players with exceptional strikeout performance, but have only played in few games. This is shown by the vertical row of dots on the left side. These players could have been exceptional players that got injured early in their career, or pitching specialists that only pitch to specific types of batters (such as the Lefty One-Out GuY aka: LOOGY) and are seldomly used.

The correlation between strikeouts and games played did increase among pitchers who have started at least 5 games, however this value still represents a weak correlation between the two statistics.

From this data analysis, it appears that left handed pitchers strike out more batters than right handed pitchers, however other factors need to be considered to make a definitive conclusion. The batting hand of the batters and number of games played may impact this data. Furthermore, over 200 pitchers were removed from the data set as their throwing hand is not listed. Perhaps comparing left handed and right handed pitcher performance to the entire pitcher population pool in itself would allow for these removed players to be included in the analysis.

Additionally, this data analysis has shown that the more games a pitcher has played in, the better their strikeout performance is. While these two data points certainly look correlated, there may be other factors that impact the strikeout performance as well as the number of games played. Injuries can reduce the amount of games played, but also the career length of a pitcher may also be impacted by the number of hits, walks, home runs, and win-loss ratio as well.
